### Video 19 — Foundations of Long/Short (Part 3): _Risk Budgets, Correlation & Portfolio Control_

— **Anton Kriel**

---

## What This Lecture Is Really About

This lecture answers a practical question professionals obsess over:

> _“How do I decide how much risk each part of the portfolio is allowed to consume?”_

Not:

- Which trade is best
    
- Which asset will move
    

But:

- How risk is **allocated, shared, and capped** across the whole system
    

This is the difference between a portfolio and a collection of trades.

---

## The Core Concept: Risk Is a Finite Resource

Anton frames risk as:

- Limited
    
- Allocatable
    
- Consumable
    

A portfolio has a **risk budget**, just like it has capital.

Once risk is consumed:

- Adding more trades does not add diversification
    
- It adds fragility
    

Retail traders think capital is the constraint.  
Professionals know **risk is the constraint**.

---

## Why Equal Capital Allocation Fails

Retail portfolios often:

- Allocate equal dollars to each position
    
- Ignore volatility differences
    
- Ignore correlation
    

This creates:

- Hidden risk concentration
    
- Uneven drawdown contributions
    
- Unpredictable portfolio behavior
    

Professionals allocate **risk**, not capital.

---

## Risk Budgeting at the Portfolio Level

A risk budget defines:

- Maximum portfolio volatility
    
- Maximum drawdown tolerance
    
- Maximum exposure to any single risk factor
    

Each position:

- Consumes part of the total risk budget
    
- Must justify its existence relative to alternatives
    

If a position:

- Adds little diversification
    
- Increases correlation
    
- Consumes excessive risk
    

It does not belong in the portfolio — even if it’s “a good idea.”

---

## Correlation Is the Silent Risk Multiplier (Revisited)

Anton deepens an earlier theme:

> Correlation is not static — it is regime-dependent.

In calm regimes:

- Assets appear independent
    
- Diversification seems abundant
    

In stress:

- Correlations converge
    
- Diversification collapses
    
- Risk budgets are breached instantly
    

Professionals assume:

- Correlation will rise when it matters
    
- Risk budgets must anticipate this
    

This is why **gross exposure must fall before correlation spikes**.

---

## Long and Short Risk Must Be Budgeted Together

A common retail error:

- Treat longs and shorts separately
    
- Assume shorts “offset” longs
    

Professional reality:

- Both consume risk
    
- Both increase gross exposure
    
- Both contribute to drawdowns when correlation rises
    

Risk budgets apply to:

- Long books
    
- Short books
    
- Factor exposures
    
- Strategy clusters
    

Everything draws from the **same finite pool**.

---

## Why More Trades Can Increase Risk

Anton emphasizes a counterintuitive point:

> Adding trades can make the portfolio _riskier_, not safer.

This happens when:

- Trades share the same drivers
    
- Correlation is underestimated
    
- Risk budgets are ignored
    

More positions ≠ more diversification  
More **independent** positions = more diversification

---

## Portfolio-Level Stop Thinking

Retail traders rely on:

- Trade-level stops
    
- Individual position exits
    

Professionals focus on:

- Portfolio drawdown limits
    
- Risk-budget breaches
    
- Exposure contraction
    

A portfolio can be “right” on many trades and still:

- Violate its risk budget
    
- Enter a death spiral
    

Portfolio controls dominate trade controls.

---

## Risk Budgets and Behavior Control

This lecture is also about **preventing behavioral failure**.

Risk budgets:

- Prevent overconfidence after wins
    
- Prevent doubling down after losses
    
- Enforce discipline mechanically
    
- Remove discretion under stress
    

They exist to protect the trader from themselves.

---

## Integration With Macro Risk Posture

Anton implicitly ties everything back:

- Leading indicators → macro risk posture
    
- Macro posture → total risk budget
    
- Risk budget → gross exposure
    
- Gross exposure → survivability
    

As conditions worsen:

- Risk budget shrinks
    
- Gross exposure contracts
    
- Strategy freedom disappears
    

This is not market timing.  
It is **risk conservation**.

---

## Key Takeaways (Part 3)

- Risk is finite and allocatable
    
- Capital allocation ≠ risk allocation
    
- Correlation multiplies risk under stress
    
- Longs and shorts share the same risk pool
    
- More trades can increase fragility
    
- Portfolio controls beat trade controls
    
- Risk budgets exist to prevent behavioral failure

---

## The Professional Entry Philosophy

Anton establishes a core rule:

> **Timing should minimize regret, not maximize precision.**

Professionals accept:

- Imperfect prices
    
- Late entries
    
- Partial fills
    
- Missed moves
    

Because the real objective is:

- Controlled exposure
    
- Emotional neutrality
    
- System compliance
    

---

## Why Coarse Timing Beats Fine Timing

Retail traders chase:

- Exact levels
    
- Candlestick patterns
    
- Micro-structures
    
- “Perfect” entries
    

Anton explains why professionals don’t:

- Fine timing requires tight stops
    
- Tight stops fail in volatility
    
- Volatility expands unpredictably
    
- Precision amplifies fragility
    

**Coarse timing**:

- Uses zones, not points
    
- Accepts noise
    
- Survives volatility expansion
    
- Reduces emotional load
    

---

## Scaling In: The Default Professional Entry

Anton emphasizes that professionals rarely enter full size at once.

**Why scale in?**

- Reduces regret
    
- Reduces psychological commitment
    
- Allows confirmation without anticipation
    
- Limits damage if wrong early
    

Scaling is not indecision — it is **risk management**.

---

## Scaling Is About Risk, Not Conviction

A critical distinction:

> **Professionals scale based on risk tolerance, not belief strength.**

Retail scaling:

- “I’m more confident now”
    
- “It’s working”
    
- “I’ll add because I’m right”
    

Professional scaling:

- Exposure remains within risk budget
    
- Volatility allows additional size
    
- Portfolio correlation remains acceptable
    

If scaling requires excitement, it is forbidden.

---

## Why Missed Trades Are Normal (and Healthy)

Anton addresses a major psychological hurdle:

> **Missing trades is not a failure.  
> Forcing trades is.**

Professionals expect:

- To miss many moves
    
- To enter late
    
- To watch markets run without them
    

This is not incompetence — it is **discipline paying rent**.

---

## Timing Does Not Improve a Bad Idea

Anton reinforces a hierarchy rule:

> **Timing never rescues bad structure.**

No entry technique:

- Fixes bad ideas
    
- Overrides hostile regimes
    
- Neutralizes correlation
    
- Saves excessive gross exposure
    

If the idea or environment is wrong, timing is irrelevant.

---

## Emotional Neutrality Is the Real Timing Edge

Anton reframes the ultimate goal:

> **Good timing feels emotionally flat.**

If you feel:

- Urgency
    
- Excitement
    
- Relief
    
- Fear of missing out
    

Then timing has failed — regardless of price.

Professionals aim for entries that feel:

- Boring
    
- Procedural
    
- Forgettable
    

Emotionless execution is the edge.

---

## The “Enter, Forget, Monitor” Model

Anton outlines a clean operational loop:

1. **Enter** when allowed
    
2. **Forget** the entry price
    
3. **Monitor** only invalidation and risk
    

Watching P&L:

- Encourages interference
    
- Encourages rationalization
    
- Encourages early exits
    

Professionals monitor **rules**, not feelings.

---

## Why Professionals Don’t Chase Breakouts

Anton clarifies a common misconception:

Professionals do not chase because:

- Chasing compresses risk/reward
    
- Crowding increases slippage
    
- Volatility spikes at inflection points
    

If an entry requires:

- Speed
    
- Aggression
    
- Perfect execution
    

It is likely **crowded or fragile**.

---

## Timing and Position Sizing (Bridge to Next Section)

Anton closes by connecting timing to what comes next:

- Timing determines _when_ exposure starts
    
- Sizing determines _how much_ exposure exists
    
- Both must respect risk budgets
    

This is why timing is taught **before** sizing:

> You cannot size what you are not allowed to enter.

---

## Key Takeaways (Timing Part 2)

- Coarse timing survives volatility
    
- Precision increases fragility
    
- Scaling reduces regret and risk
    
- Missed trades are normal
    
- Timing cannot fix bad ideas
    
- Emotionless entries are correct entries
    
- Monitoring rules beats watching P&L

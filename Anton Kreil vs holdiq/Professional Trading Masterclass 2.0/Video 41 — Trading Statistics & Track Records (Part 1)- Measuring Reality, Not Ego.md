### Video 41 — _Trading Statistics & Track Records (Part 1): Measuring Reality, Not Ego_

— **Anton Kriel**

---

## What This Lecture Is Really About

Anton reframes statistics immediately:

> **Trading statistics are not for marketing.  
> They are for self-surveillance.**

Retail traders use stats to:

- Prove they are good
    
- Impress others
    
- Justify confidence
    
- Hide uncertainty
    

Professionals use stats to:

- Detect decay
    
- Detect behavioral drift
    
- Detect regime mismatch
    
- Decide when to stop trading
    

Statistics exist to **protect capital**, not to boost self-image.

---

## The Core Problem: Humans Lie With Numbers

Anton makes an uncomfortable point:

> **Most traders don’t lack data — they misuse it.**

Common statistical abuse:

- Cherry-picking time windows
    
- Ignoring drawdowns
    
- Averaging away pain
    
- Focusing on win rate
    
- Ignoring sequence risk
    
- Hiding tail losses
    

Numbers become a story, not a mirror.

---

## Why P&L Alone Is Meaningless

Anton is blunt:

> **Profit does not equal skill.**

P&L is:

- Path-dependent
    
- Regime-dependent
    
- Sample-size sensitive
    
- Easily distorted by leverage
    

A trader can make money:

- By luck
    
- By regime alignment
    
- By excessive risk
    
- By hidden convexity
    

Professionals treat raw returns with suspicion.

---

## The Purpose of a Track Record

Anton defines a professional track record as:

> **Evidence that a process behaves as expected across time, stress, and uncertainty.**

A real track record answers:

- Does the system survive drawdowns?
    
- Does risk expand when it shouldn’t?
    
- Does behavior change under pressure?
    
- Does performance degrade gracefully?
    
- Does the trader follow rules consistently?
    

Returns are secondary.

---

## Why Short Track Records Are Dangerous

Anton emphasizes sample size:

> **Most “track records” are statistically meaningless.**

Problems with short histories:

- Overrepresent luck
    
- Underrepresent tail events
    
- Miss regime shifts
    
- Inflate confidence
    

Professionals distrust:

- Months of data
    
- Single market cycles
    
- Smooth equity curves
    

They want to see **stress**.

---

## Sequence Risk: The Silent Killer

Anton introduces (or reinforces) sequence risk:

> **The order of wins and losses matters more than averages.**

Two strategies with identical stats can:

- Produce radically different outcomes
    
- Trigger different behaviors
    
- Cause different drawdown paths
    

Professionals track:

- Loss clusters
    
- Drawdown depth
    
- Drawdown duration
    
- Recovery time
    

These determine survivability.

---

## Why Win Rate Is a Trap

Anton attacks a retail obsession:

> **Win rate is one of the least informative trading statistics.**

High win rate often implies:

- Negative convexity
    
- Small wins, large losses
    
- Hidden tail risk
    

Low win rate strategies:

- Can be superior
    
- If losses are small and controlled
    
- And wins are asymmetric
    

Professionals care about **loss behavior**, not win frequency.

---

## Risk-Adjusted Metrics (Used Carefully)

Anton does not dismiss metrics like:

- Sharpe
    
- Sortino
    
- Expectancy
    

But he warns:

- They are summaries, not truth
    
- They hide tails
    
- They average away pain
    
- They break in regime change
    

Metrics must be read alongside **raw drawdowns**.

---

## Behavior Shows Up in the Data

Anton emphasizes an important insight:

> **Statistics reveal behavior, not just performance.**

Signs of behavioral failure in stats:

- Sudden volatility increases
    
- Position size creep
    
- Correlation spikes
    
- Drawdown acceleration
    
- Performance asymmetry after wins/losses
    

A track record is a **psychological X-ray**.

---

## Why Professionals Prefer Ugly but Honest Records

Anton makes a strong value judgment:

> **A messy track record is often more trustworthy than a smooth one.**

Smooth curves usually indicate:

- Overfitting
    
- Excessive leverage
    
- Hidden risk selling
    
- Survivorship bias
    

Professionals want to see:

- Pain
    
- Mistakes
    
- Recovery
    
- Stability under stress
    

Reality is not smooth.

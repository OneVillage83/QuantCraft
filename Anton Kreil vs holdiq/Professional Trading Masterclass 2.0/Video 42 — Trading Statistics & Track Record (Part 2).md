## Professional Trading Masterclass 2.0

### Video 42 — _Trading Statistics & Track Record (Part 2): Decision-Making From Data_

— **Anton Kriel**

---

## What This Lecture Is Really About

Anton answers the practical question:

> **“Once I have statistics — what am I actually supposed to _do_ with them?”**

Retail traders collect stats to:

- Feel reassured
    
- Justify risk
    
- Defend decisions
    
- Market themselves
    

Professionals use stats to:

- **Change behavior**
    
- **Reduce exposure**
    
- **Disable strategies**
    
- **Stop trading when necessary**
    

If statistics do not alter decisions, they are decoration.

---

## The Professional Use of a Track Record

Anton defines the real function of a track record:

> **A track record is a control system, not a scorecard.**

Its job is to answer:

- Is the system behaving as expected?
    
- Is risk being expressed correctly?
    
- Is behavior degrading under stress?
    
- Is the environment still compatible?
    

If the answer is unclear, risk must be reduced — not increased.

---

## Expectation vs Reality (The Core Comparison)

Professionals always compare:

- **Expected behavior** (from design)  
    vs
    
- **Observed behavior** (from data)
    

Key questions:

- Are drawdowns within expected bounds?
    
- Is volatility consistent with assumptions?
    
- Are losses clustering more than expected?
    
- Is recovery time lengthening?
    
- Is correlation creeping up?
    

Deviation matters more than raw performance.

---

## Why You Must Define “Normal” in Advance

Anton stresses a critical discipline:

> **You cannot evaluate performance unless you defined “normal” beforehand.**

Professionals predefine:

- Acceptable drawdown depth
    
- Acceptable drawdown duration
    
- Expected win/loss distribution
    
- Expected volatility range
    
- Expected behavior after wins/losses
    

Without this:

- Every loss feels abnormal
    
- Every win feels validating
    
- Decisions become emotional
    

Stats must be judged against **pre-committed expectations**.

---

## When Professionals Reduce Risk (Even if Profitable)

Anton makes a counterintuitive point:

> **Professionals often reduce risk while making money.**

Reasons include:

- Volatility exceeding expectations
    
- Drawdowns occurring too quickly
    
- Win rate masking tail risk
    
- Correlation rising silently
    
- Behavior changing post-success
    

Profit does not grant permission.  
Deviation triggers restriction.

---

## When Professionals Stop Trading Entirely

Anton is explicit:

> **Stopping is a professional decision, not a failure.**

Professionals stop trading when:

- Behavior deviates from design
    
- Drawdowns exceed expected duration
    
- Recovery characteristics change
    
- Edge degradation is suspected
    
- Regime compatibility disappears
    

Stopping preserves:

- Capital
    
- Psychological stability
    
- Optionality
    
- System integrity
    

Retail traders trade through breakdowns.  
Professionals pause.

---

## Why More Data Is Not Always Better

Anton cautions against over-analysis:

> **More statistics do not automatically produce better decisions.**

Too many metrics:

- Create noise
    
- Enable cherry-picking
    
- Encourage rationalization
    
- Delay necessary action
    

Professionals track **a small set of high-signal metrics**, obsessively.

---

## The Small Set of Metrics That Actually Matter

While Anton avoids giving a rigid list, the emphasis is clear:

Professionals prioritize:

- Drawdown depth
    
- Drawdown duration
    
- Volatility of returns
    
- Loss clustering
    
- Risk per trade drift
    
- Correlation creep
    
- Post-win / post-loss behavior shifts
    

If these are stable, returns are secondary.

---

## Why Stats Must Be Read Conservatively

Anton emphasizes asymmetry:

> **You only need statistics to be wrong once to destroy years of work.**

Therefore:

- Interpret stats pessimistically
    
- Assume luck over skill
    
- Assume regimes change
    
- Assume decay before collapse
    

Optimism kills traders.  
Skepticism keeps them alive.

---

## Track Records as Early-Warning Systems

Anton reframes the ideal outcome:

> **The best track record tells you to stop _before_ the damage is obvious.**

If stats only confirm failure after a blow-up:

- They were useless
    

Professionals want:

- Early warning
    
- False positives over false negatives
    
- Inconvenient caution
    

Being early is survivable.  
Being late is fatal.

---

## Why Professionals Prefer Short Leashes

Anton reinforces a cultural difference:

Retail mindset:

> “Give it more time.”

Professional mindset:

> “Why should this still be working?”

Short leashes:

- Prevent denial
    
- Prevent escalation
    
- Prevent identity attachment
    

Long leashes destroy systems quietly.

---

## Key Takeaways (Statistics Part 2)

- Track records exist to change decisions
    
- Expectation vs reality matters more than returns
    
- “Normal” must be defined in advance
    
- Profit does not justify increased risk
    
- Stopping is professional behavior
    
- Few metrics matter — track them relentlessly
    
- Stats are early-warning systems, not validation
